# Section 1 — Mission Preparation

Before leaving the base camp or survey vehicle, confirm that the full survey kit is available.

### Equipment Checklist

☐ handheld GPS unit  
☐ geological hammer  
☐ sample bags (labeled)  
☐ portable XRF analyzer  
☐ field notebook  
☐ camera or imaging device  
☐ soil auger (if subsurface sampling planned)

Optional equipment:

☐ drone mapping unit  
☐ portable spectrometer  
☐ additional battery packs

If the mission involves multi-day surveying, extra water and emergency supplies should be packed separately.

---

# Section 2 — Site Arrival Procedure

Upon reaching the survey location:

1. Record the GPS coordinates.
2. Note the general terrain characteristics.
3. Identify any visible mineral outcrops or unusual rock formations.
4. Take initial site photographs.

Field teams often sketch the site layout in their notebooks before collecting samples.

Example field note format:


Site ID: GS-2026-17
Location: ridge line above eastern valley
Rock type: mostly sedimentary layers
Weather: clear, moderate wind


These initial observations help contextualize later laboratory analysis.

---

# Section 3 — Surface Sampling

Surface rock sampling should focus on areas showing distinct mineral patterns.

### Sampling Steps

Procedure:

1. Identify exposed rock face
2. Clean loose debris
3. Strike rock with hammer
4. Collect fresh fragment

Avoid collecting heavily weathered fragments whenever possible.

Place samples in labeled bags immediately.

---

### Sample Label Format

Each sample should include the following information.

| Field | Example |
|------|---------|
| Site ID | GS-2026-17 |
| Sample Number | S03 |
| Date | 12-Apr |
| Collector | J. Ortiz |

Incomplete labeling is a common source of confusion during later analysis.

# Section 4 — Subsurface Soil Sampling

Subsurface samples may reveal mineral deposits not visible at the surface.

Procedure:

☐ insert soil auger vertically  
☐ rotate until reaching desired depth  
☐ extract soil column  
☐ separate layers if visible  

Typical sampling depth ranges between **20–60 cm**, though deeper sampling may be required in certain environments.

---

### Layer Description Example

| Depth | Soil Type | Color |
|------|-----------|------|
| 0–10 cm | sandy | light brown |
| 10–30 cm | clay rich | dark brown |
| 30–50 cm | compact sediment | grey |

Unexpected color changes may indicate mineral variation.

---

# Section 5 — Portable XRF Analysis

Portable XRF analyzers allow quick estimation of elemental composition in the field.

Typical elements detected include:

- iron (Fe)
- copper (Cu)
- zinc (Zn)
- nickel (Ni)

### Measurement Procedure


place analyzer flat against sample surface

trigger scan

wait for analysis result

record element percentages


Readings may vary depending on surface roughness.

Multiple scans are recommended for reliability.

# Section 6 — Field Documentation

Accurate documentation is critical for later geological interpretation.

Recommended records include:

- photographs of sampling locations
- sketches of geological layers
- notes about rock texture or color
- environmental observations

Some teams use digital tablets for mapping, though traditional notebooks remain common in remote areas.

---

# Section 7 — Sample Storage and Transport

Collected samples must be stored carefully to avoid contamination or misidentification.

Basic storage guidelines:

☐ seal sample bags properly  
☐ keep samples dry  
☐ avoid mixing materials from different sites  
☐ store fragile samples separately

At the end of the day, samples should be logged into the central inventory sheet.

---

# Section 8 — Safety Considerations

Field geological surveys often take place in remote environments.

Potential hazards include:

- unstable rock surfaces
- sudden weather changes
- wildlife encounters
- equipment failure

Field teams should maintain radio communication with the base camp whenever possible.

---

# Section 9 — End-of-Day Procedure

Before leaving the survey area:

1. confirm that all samples are labeled
2. verify GPS coordinates for each sampling site
3. photograph any remaining geological features
4. check that no equipment is left behind

A short daily summary should be written in the field notebook.

Example entry:


Day summary:
Collected 11 rock samples and 4 soil cores.
Notable copper traces detected near ridge outcrop.
Further sampling recommended along eastern slope.
